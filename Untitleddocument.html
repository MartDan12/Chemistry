                <h2>Atomic Structure, Quantum Orbitals & Periodicity</h2>

                <p>The description of atomic configurations transitions in Senior Secondary 2 from the planetary electron shells proposed by Niels Bohr into the non-deterministic models of modern quantum mechanics. The exact path of an electron cannot be localized precisely; instead, it is mapped into fields of high probability within designated atomic orbitals.</p>

               

                <h3>Quantum Subshells: s, p, d, f Orbitals</h3>

                <p>Electrons organize themselves within sub-energy levels around the nucleus based on mathematical quantum limits. Each main energy level shell ($n$) contains subshells designated as $s, p, d,$ and $f$. Each distinct orbital can hold a maximum configuration capacity of two electrons with opposing spins.</p>

                <ul>

                    <li><strong>s-subshell:</strong> Spherical symmetry, contains 1 orbital (holds up to 2 electrons).</li>

                    <li><strong>p-subshell:</strong> Dumbbell-shaped, contains 3 orthogonal orbitals ($p_x, p_y, p_z$, holds up to 6 electrons).</li>

                    <li><strong>d-subshell:</strong> Complex geometry, contains 5 degenerate orbitals (holds up to 10 electrons).</li>

                </ul>

                <h3>The Core Quantum Electronic Principles</h3>

                <p>To write out electronic structures accurately, three fundamental rules must be satisfied simultaneously:</p>

                <ol>

                    <li><strong>The Aufbau Principle:</strong> Electrons sequentially fill atomic orbitals starting from the lowest available energy ground levels before occupying higher levels.</li>

                    <li><strong>Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity:</strong> For degenerate orbitals (orbitals with identical energy levels, such as the $p$-orbitals), electrons must occupy each orbital singly with parallel spins before they begin to pair up.</li>

                    <li><strong>The Pauli Exclusion Principle:</strong> No two electrons inside a single atom can share an identical set of four quantum coordinates. This means an orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons, and they must have opposite spins.</li>

                </ol>

                <div class="formula-display">

                    Oxygen (O, Z=8): 1s² 2s² 2p⁴<br>

                    Sodium (Na, Z=11): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹<br>

                    Scandium (Sc, Z=21): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d¹

                </div>

                <h3>Advanced Periodic Trends Analysis</h3>

                <p><strong>Atomic Radius:</strong> Moving horizontally across a period, the atomic radius decreases. This occurs because protons are added to the nucleus one by one, increasing the positive nuclear charge. This stronger pull draws the same outer electron shells closer inward. Conversely, moving down a group, the atomic radius increases due to the addition of entirely new physical energy levels, which shield the outer electrons from the nucleus.</p>

                <p><strong>Ionization Energy (IE):</strong> The minimum energy required to completely remove the most loosely bound electron from an isolated gaseous atom in its ground state. This value spikes across a period due to tighter atomic radii holding valence shells firmly, and drops down a vertical group due to increased atomic shielding.</p>

                <h2>Chemical Bonding: Molecular and Intermolecular Formations</h2>

                <p>Chemical bonding describes the attractive electrostatic forces that hold atoms or ions together to form stable chemical structures. Atoms interact to achieve a stable electronic arrangement, typically aiming for an outer octet of valence electrons similar to the electron configurations of noble gases.</p>

               

                <h3>Primary Intramolecular Bonds</h3>

                <p><strong>1. Electrovalent (Ionic) Bonding:</strong> This bond forms through the complete transfer of valence electrons from a highly electropositive metal atom to a highly electronegative non-metal atom. This creates distinct cations and anions held together by strong, multi-directional electrostatic forces within a stable crystal lattice. A classic example is the reaction between Sodium and Chlorine to produce Sodium Chloride ($\text{NaCl}$):</p>

                <div class="formula-display">

                    2Na (1s²2s²2p⁶3s¹) + Cl₂ (...3s²3p⁵) &rarr; 2Na⁺ (1s²2s²2p⁶) + 2Cl⁻ (1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p⁶)

                </div>

                <p><strong>2. Covalent Bonding:</strong> This bond forms through the mutual sharing of pairs of valence electrons between electronegative non-metal atoms. If the atoms have different electronegativities, the electron density shifts toward the more electronegative atom, creating a polar covalent bond (such as in $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ or $\text{HCl}$).</p>

               

                <p><strong>3. Coordinate (Dative) Covalent Bonding:</strong> A variation of covalent bonding where the shared pair of electrons is provided entirely by one of the bonding atoms. The reacting species must consist of a donor atom with an unshared lone pair of electrons and an acceptor atom that is electron-deficient. This mechanism is responsible for the formation of the Ammonium ion ($\text{NH}_4^+$) from Ammonia ($\text{NH}_3$) and a hydrogen ion ($\text{H}^+$):</p>

                <div class="formula-display">

                    NH₃ (Lone Pair Donor) + H⁺ (Acceptor) &rarr; [NH₄]⁺

                </div>

                <h3>Secondary Intermolecular Forces</h3>

                <p><strong>Hydrogen Bonding:</strong> This is a strong intermolecular dipole-dipole attraction that occurs when a hydrogen atom is covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom with small atomic radius, specifically Fluorine, Oxygen, or Nitrogen ($\text{F, O, N}$). Although weaker than covalent bonds, hydrogen bonding significantly impacts physical properties. For example, it explains why water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$) has an anomalously high boiling point of 100°C and exists as a liquid at room temperature, while hydrogen sulfide ($\text{H}_2\text{S}$), which has a higher molecular mass but lacks hydrogen bonding, is a gas.</p>

                <h2>Quantitative Solubility and Dynamic Saturation</h2>

                <p>The solubility of a solute is the maximum chemical quantity (expressed in grams or moles) that can completely dissolve in $1\text{ dm}^3$ ($1000\text{ cm}^3$) of a given solvent at a specific, controlled temperature to create a saturated solution in dynamic equilibrium.</p>

               

                <p>A solution is **saturated** when the rate at which the solute dissolves matches the rate at which it crystallizes back out of solution. If a hot saturated solution cools down, the solvent can no longer hold that amount of solute, causing the excess mass to precipitate out as crystals.</p>

                <div class="proof-box">

                    <h4>📝 Rigorous Textbook Worked Example</h4>

                    <p><strong>Problem Statement:</strong> An experimental sample of $12.5\text{ g}$ of pure Potassium Trioxonitrate(V) ($\text{KNO}_3$) was found to completely saturate $40\text{ cm}^3$ of distilled water at $35^\circ\text{C}$. Calculate:</p>

                    <ol>

                        <li>The mass solubility of $\text{KNO}_3$ in $\text{g/dm}^3$ at this temperature.</li>

                        <li>The molar solubility of the salt in $\text{mol/dm}^3$.</li>

                    </ol>

                    <p><strong>Relative Atomic Masses [RAM]:</strong> $\text{K}
